For years there's been a quiet two-tier system in business software. If you had a six-figure budget and an IT department, you got proper systems — built for how you work, wired together, watched over. If you didn't, you got a shelf of off-the-shelf apps that almost fit, and a spreadsheet holding the whole thing together with willpower.

## The spreadsheet is a symptom

When we walk into a small business, the spreadsheet is almost always there. It's the seam where two systems that refuse to talk get stitched together by a human, by hand, every week. Someone re-keys the numbers. Someone chases the approval. Someone remembers the pricing.

That spreadsheet isn't a failing. It's a rational response to being priced out of the alternative. The proper version — a system that just does it — used to cost more than the problem.

## What changed

Two things. The technology to build custom systems got dramatically cheaper and faster, and AI agents can now do the reading, deciding and drafting that used to need a person at a desk. Put those together and the economics flip. A job that would have justified a £60,000 platform can be solved for a fraction of that — and *stay* solved, watched over for a monthly fee smaller than a part-time hire.

## The catch nobody mentions

Cheaper tools don't help if the person building them still charges enterprise rates for enterprise overhead. That's the bit we changed on purpose: we run our own back office on agents, so we're lean enough to publish sensible prices and mean them. An Audit from £450. A Sprint from £1,950. Numbers on a page, not a discovery-call ritual.

There's no good reason a care provider in Bradford or a manufacturer in Manchester should have worse systems than anyone else. The gap was always about cost, and the cost just fell through the floor.
